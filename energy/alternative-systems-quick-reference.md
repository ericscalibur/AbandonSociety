# Alternative Energy Systems Quick Reference Guide

*Practical implementation guide for innovative energy generation and conversion systems*

## Biogas Systems (Anaerobic Digestion)

### System Sizing Quick Calculator
**Daily Gas Production Formula:**
- Fresh cow manure: 1-1.5 ft³ gas per pound
- Food waste: 3-4 ft³ gas per pound
- Human waste: 0.5-1 ft³ gas per pound
- Retention time: 15-30 days (temperature dependent)

**Example Calculation:**
- Family of 4 producing 20 lbs organic waste/day
- 20 lbs × 2.5 ft³/lb = 50 ft³ biogas daily
- Cooking needs: 4-8 ft³/day per person = 16-32 ft³/day
- System produces 150-200% of cooking gas needs

### DIY Digester Construction
**Simple Barrel Digester:**
- 55-gallon drum with gas collection dome
- PVC gas outlet with water seal
- Slurry input and output pipes
- Total cost: $200-400
- Construction time: 1-2 weekends

**Underground Fixed Dome:**
- 200-500 gallon capacity
- Ferrocement or concrete construction
- 20-30 year lifespan
- Total cost: $800-2000
- Construction time: 2-4 weeks

### Gas Utilization
**Direct Combustion:**
- Cooking stoves: 60-70% efficiency
- Water heating: 50-60% efficiency
- Space heating: 40-50% efficiency
- BTU content: 600-650 BTU/ft³

**Engine Generator Applications:**
- Small generators: 15-25 ft³ per kWh
- Efficiency: 25-35% electrical
- Combined heat and power: 75-85% total
- Requires gas cleaning and pressure regulation

## Wood Gasification (Producer Gas)

### Gasifier Sizing
**Power Output Calculation:**
- 1 pound dry wood = 7,000-8,500 BTU
- Producer gas = 120-200 BTU/ft³
- Engine efficiency = 25-30%
- Result: 4-6 pounds wood per kWh electricity

**Gasifier Types Comparison:**
```
Type        | Tar Content | Engine Suitable | Construction
------------|-------------|-----------------|-------------
Updraft     | High        | No             | Simple
Downdraft   | Low         | Yes            | Moderate  
Cross-draft | Very Low    | Yes            | Complex
```

### Simple Downdraft Gasifier Design
**Core Components:**
- Fire pot: 12-18" diameter steel
- Air nozzles: 4-6 tuyeres at throat
- Reduction zone: 18-24" below throat
- Gas exit: Bottom with cooling/cleaning
- Fuel hopper: 2-4 hours fuel capacity

**Fuel Requirements:**
- Moisture content: 10-20% (15% optimal)
- Size: 0.5-2" chunks
- Density: Hardwoods preferred
- Ash content: <5% for clean operation

### Engine Modifications for Producer Gas
**Required Changes:**
- Advance ignition timing 5-10 degrees
- Increase compression ratio if possible
- Install mixer at air intake
- Add governor for load changes
- Install safety shutoffs

**Performance Expectations:**
- Power reduction: 30-40% vs gasoline
- Fuel consumption: 4-6 lbs wood per hour per HP
- Startup time: 10-20 minutes
- Maintenance: Higher due to tar and particulates

## Sterling Engine Systems

### Heat Source Requirements
**Temperature Differentials:**
- Minimum: 200°F difference
- Good performance: 400-600°F difference
- Optimal: 800-1000°F difference
- Maximum metal temp: 1200-1400°F

**Heat Sources by Temperature:**
```
Source              | Temperature | Power Density
--------------------|-------------|---------------
Solar concentrator  | 1000-2000°F | High
Wood combustion     | 1400-2000°F | High
Waste heat         | 200-600°F   | Low
Compost            | 130-160°F   | Very Low
```

### DIY Sterling Engine Construction
**Alpha Configuration (Two Pistons):**
- Power cylinder: Hot end
- Displacer cylinder: Cold end
- Connecting rod: 90° phase difference
- Regenerator: Steel wool or mesh
- Difficulty: Moderate to High

**Beta Configuration (Single Cylinder):**
- Power piston: Bottom of cylinder
- Displacer: Top of cylinder
- Linkage: Rhombic or crank drive
- Regenerator: Built into displacer
- Difficulty: High

**Gamma Configuration (Separate Cylinders):**
- Power cylinder: Connected via pipe
- Displacer cylinder: Hot/cold ends
- Easier machining: Separate components
- Lower performance: Connection losses
- Difficulty: Moderate

### Performance Expectations
**Power Output:**
- Small engines: 10-100 watts
- Medium engines: 100W-5kW
- Large engines: 5-50kW
- Efficiency: 15-35% (Carnot limit dependent)

**Maintenance Requirements:**
- Seal replacement: Annually
- Regenerator cleaning: As needed
- Bearing lubrication: Monthly
- Performance monitoring: Continuous

## Pyrolysis Systems

### Plastic-to-Diesel Pyrolysis
**Feedstock Preparation:**
- Clean sorted plastics (PE, PP, PS preferred)
- Remove labels, caps, and contaminants
- Shred to 1/4" to 1/2" pieces
- Moisture content: <2%

**Process Parameters:**
- Temperature: 800-900°F (425-480°C)
- Pressure: Slight vacuum preferred
- Residence time: 1-4 hours
- Catalyst: Zeolite improves quality
- Yield: 60-80% liquid fuel by weight

**Simple Reactor Design:**
- Steel vessel: 55-gallon drum minimum
- Heating system: Propane or wood-fired
- Condenser: Coiled tube in water bath
- Collection: Sealed containers
- Safety: Temperature control, pressure relief

**Fuel Quality:**
- Specific gravity: 0.82-0.92
- Calorific value: 40-44 MJ/kg
- Sulfur content: <0.1%
- Flash point: 35-60°C
- Usage: Diesel engines, heating, generators

### Biomass Pyrolysis (Biochar Production)
**Process Options:**
```
Type   | Temperature | Time    | Biochar Yield | Applications
-------|-------------|---------|---------------|---------------
Slow   | 600-700°F   | 2-6 hrs | 35-45%       | Soil amendment
Fast   | 900-1000°F  | Seconds | 15-25%       | Bio-oil production
```

**Simple Retort Design:**
- Inner chamber: Biomass container
- Outer chamber: Combustion zone
- Gas ports: Volatiles combustion
- Cooling system: Water jacket
- Capacity: 50-500 pounds per batch

## Thermoelectric Generation

### Thermoelectric Module Selection
**Seebeck Effect Applications:**
- Temperature difference creates voltage
- No moving parts
- Low maintenance
- 3-8% efficiency typical
- Direct DC output

**Module Specifications:**
```
Temperature Diff | Power Output | Efficiency | Applications
-----------------|--------------|------------|----------------
50°F            | 0.1-0.5W     | 2-3%      | LED lighting
150°F           | 1-5W         | 4-5%      | Radio, phone
300°F           | 5-25W        | 5-7%      | Small tools
500°F+          | 25-100W      | 6-8%      | Battery charging
```

### Wood Stove Generator Design
**Installation Method:**
- Mount modules on stove surface
- Heat sink on cold side (fan cooled)
- Thermal interface material
- Electrical connections in parallel
- Temperature monitoring

**Expected Performance:**
- 20-50 watts typical for wood stove
- 12V battery charging capability
- LED lighting and small electronics
- Fan and circulator pump power
- Emergency communication systems

## Compost Heat Recovery

### System Design Principles
**Heat Generation:**
- Thermophilic bacteria active 104-160°F
- Peak temperatures: 130-160°F
- Duration: 2-6 months depending on size
- Heat output: 10-40 BTU/hr per cubic foot

**Heat Extraction Methods:**
- Water coils: 1/2" to 1" pipe loops
- Air circulation: Perforated pipes
- Direct coupling: Against greenhouse walls
- Thermal siphon: Natural circulation

### Water Heating Applications
**Coil Design:**
- Pipe material: PEX, copper, or polyethylene
- Coil spacing: 18-24" between loops
- Total length: 100-300 feet per cubic yard
- Flow rate: 0.5-2 GPM for optimal heat transfer

**Temperature Expectations:**
- Water input: 50-70°F
- Water output: 90-130°F
- System efficiency: 20-40%
- Seasonal availability: Spring through fall

**Installation Process:**
1. Build compost pile in layers
2. Install coil loops every 18"
3. Connect to insulated tank
4. Add circulation pump (optional)
5. Monitor temperature and adjust flow

### Greenhouse Heating
**Air Circulation System:**
- 4-6" perforated drainage pipe
- Fan-forced or natural circulation
- Temperature differential: 20-50°F
- Distribution ducts throughout greenhouse

**Thermal Mass Integration:**
- Water barrels for heat storage
- Concrete or stone thermal mass
- Phase change materials
- Day/night temperature buffering

## Safety Considerations Summary

### General Safety Rules
- Always have fire suppression equipment
- Install temperature and pressure monitoring
- Provide adequate ventilation for all gas systems
- Use appropriate materials for high temperatures
- Plan emergency shutdown procedures

### Specific Hazards
**Biogas Systems:**
- Hydrogen sulfide toxicity
- Methane explosion risk
- Oxygen displacement in confined spaces
- Pathogen exposure from raw feedstock

**Gasification Systems:**
- Carbon monoxide poisoning
- Hot surfaces and fire hazards
- Tar and particulate exposure
- Explosive gas mixtures

**Pyrolysis Systems:**
- Toxic vapor release
- High-pressure steam
- Hot oil handling
- Chemical contamination

### Emergency Procedures
1. Know location of all shutoffs
2. Post emergency contact numbers
3. Train all operators in safety procedures
4. Maintain first aid supplies
5. Regular safety drills and equipment checks

Remember: Start with the simplest, safest systems first. Build experience and confidence before attempting more complex installations. Always prioritize safety over performance, and never operate systems without proper monitoring and safety equipment.
